A **work order** is the bridge between sales and operations. When an estimate is released, its scope becomes a work order — the authoritative instruction list that tells the crew exactly what to do, and where.

## What a work order contains

<Steps>
  <Step title="Client & property">
    The work order links back to the **client** and the **property**, carrying forward all the site context (access, hazards, special instructions).
  </Step>
  <Step title="Line items">
    Each piece of scope becomes a **work order item** — the item name, unit, quantity, and category. These come from the sold estimate.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Work areas">
    Items are organized into **work areas** (for example _kitchen_, _primary bath_, _exterior_) so the crew knows which zone each task belongs to.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Crew notes">
    Notes from the estimate carry over and stay visible to the crew on site.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Why work areas matter

Grouping items by **work area** turns a flat price quote into an executable plan. A crew lead can see, at a glance, everything that needs to happen in each zone of the property — which makes it far easier to sequence the work and assign people.

<Info>
  A work order describes **what** must be done. The schedule and crew assignments — the **when** and **who** — are handled in the next stage by the job and its plan.
</Info>

## From work order to job

Once work is authorized, the system creates a **job** to execute it. The job references both the work order and the originating estimate, so cost and scope stay traceable end to end.
